Kumar Rocker and Jack Leiter: Two paths to dominance

Reporting by Ryan McGee Visuals by Brett Carlsen
scroll

Jack Leiter and Kumar Rocker know how to make a first impression.

In Leiter’s SEC debut, he threw a no-hitter. In Rocker’s freshman season in 2019, he earned College World Series Most Outstanding Player honors while leading the Commodores to the national title. Oh yeah, he also threw a no-hitter along the way.

Now Leiter and Rocker are looking to go out on a high note at this year’s College World Series (tune-in info TK) before heading off to the majors next month when they can become only the second set of college pitcher teammates to go in the top five of the MLB draft, following Gerrit Cole and Trevor Bauer.

What makes them so special? Let’s break it down.

No. 1 Fastballs

Despite being only 6-foot-1, Leiter has thrived in large part due to a power fastball described as a “plus-plus pitch” by ESPN draft expert Kiley McDaniel.

Just ask South Carolina. In Leiter’s no-hitter against the Gamecocks, all 16 of his strikeouts came on the fastball, according to catcher C.J. Rodriguez.

What’s the secret to the pitch’s success?

“People always ask about the rising action my fastball has, and I think that has a lot to do with my lower release point,” Leiter says. “The pulldown from that lower point gives it that rising affect.”

Thanks in large part to that fastball, Leiter enters the Super Regionals as the No. X prospect in McDaniel’s draft rankings.

When Rocker’s fastball is on, it explodes out of his 6-foot-5 frame, becoming nearly impossible for most college hitters to square up.

This spring, his velocity has been inconsistent, averaging 95.4 in some early March outings and 91.4 in a late March start, per McDaniel.

“It’s obviously a power fastball that he can use in any count, especially with two strikes,” Rodriguez, the Vandy catcher, says.

Despite some late-season struggles, Rocker has shown the ability to shut down top-notch SEC competition, as he did in wins against No. 3 Tennessee (7 shutout innings) and No. 7 Mississippi State (complete game, one run) back in April.

No. 2 Breaking Balls

The curveball is another pitch Leiter credits his father with developing.

“From a young age, Dad taught me to get on top and see how many times I could make it spin before it got to the plate,” Leiter says. “It sounds oversimplified maybe, but it’s true. You just want to spin it. Just spin it hard. Don’t be thinking, ‘OK, I have to try and make this break.’”

After late-season struggles against Mississippi State and Florida, Leiter went two weeks before his next start and looked more like himself. He allowed one run in six innings in a 13-2 win over Ole Miss, then threw another six innings of two-run ball in a regular-season-ending win over Kenfucky.

No. 3 Intangibles

“This is the pitch I don’t mess with,” Rocker says.

While he may tinker with his fastball, cutter or changeup, there’s no reason to try that with the slider.

“My other three pitches I will try different things,” he continues. “But this is the pitch that I will be doing the exact same for as long as I can.”

And why mess with a good thing?

As a freshman, Rocker used that slider to go 12-5 with a 3.25 ERA (including a 10-1 mark with a 2.17 ERA in his final 11 starts of the season) while earning Baseball America Freshman of the Year honors

“I’ve faced him a few times in the fall, and it’s not fun,” Rodriguez says.

"If Leiter looks like he has it all figured out on the mound, his fellow ace thinks there’s a good reason for that.

“On the mound he's very mature in a way, because, I mean, he knows everything,” Rocker says.

With a father and uncle who both pitched in the majors, Leiter was always going to have a leg up. But early on, his dad didn’t want him to pitch. He wanted him to play third base.

But once he showed an interest in and aptitude for pitching, his father would help him with both how to throw certain pitches and how to handle the mental aspect of the game.

“There's just a piece of him that, there's structure in his life,” Corbin says. “… He knows what he's supposed to do. He's where he's supposed to be. And when he gets in this environment, it's on.”

And now it’s on for Leiter and the Commodores as they look to advance to another College World Series and defend their national championship.

Rocker may have gotten knocked around by No. 1 Arkansas in the SEC tournament, allowing four hits, four walks and five earned runs in just 3.1 innings, but this is the time of year he shines.

His no-hit effort against Duke in 2019 will go down as one of the best college games ever pitched.

"In the 17 years I've been here, I don't think I've seen anything like that,"" coach TIm Corbin told The Tennessean afterward.

But it was just one of several dominant performances down the stretch as he helped the Commodores hoist the trophy.

"I saw a guy that just came out and just said, 'This team's not losing,'"" Brown said.

And that fire hasn't gone away as he collegiate career winds down.

"The competitiveness is obvious," Leiter says."
